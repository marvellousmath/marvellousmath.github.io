Clay-Water Interaction

Water

Water molecules are composed by one atom of oxygen, negatively charged, and two atoms of hydrogen, positively charged. The atoms are bonded forming and angle of 105 degree. As a consequence, water molecules are strong dipoles.

Possible mechanisms of clay-water interaction

  • Dipole: The positive pole of the water molecule is attracted by the negative charge of the clay.
  • Ion Hydration: The cations dispersed around a clay particle bond to the oxygen atom of the water molecules.
  • Hydrogen bonds: One electron is shared by the clay particle and one atom of hydrogen of the water molecules.
  • Van der Waals forces: mass attraction

The colloidal nature of clay

  • Particle size < 1μm
  • Surface area > 25 m²/g
  • Behavior influenced by surface forces

Electrophoresis: water with clay particles dispersed in it is submitted to an electrical field. Because the clay particles are negatively charged, they are repelled by the negative pole and attracted by the positive pole.

Electro-osmosis: a sample of saturated clay is submitted to an electrical field. The cations dispersed around the clay particles are repelled by the positive pole and attracted by the negative pole.

Ion distribution in a clay particle

  • Cations and anionare distributed adjacent to a clay particle;
  • Cation are distributed based on the balancing of two forces:
    • Electrostatic attraction from the negative charge of the clay;
    • Escaping tendency due to diffusion. That is, the cations repelled each other and are attracted by the anions.

Difuse double layer - DDL

The cations form a layer shrouding the clay particle. There is a high concentration of cations very close to the clay particle and the concentration decreases as the distance from the clay particle increases. Thus, it is useful to determine the center of gravity of this cloud of cations. This is the defeiniton of the thickness of the DDL.

  • The DDL exchangeable;

  • The DDL does not affect the structure of the clay particles

  • The ion exchange happens very fast for low PI clays

  • Ions with greater valance and low radius have preference:

    \(Na^{+}<K^{+}<Mg^{++}<Ca^{++}<Al^{+++}<Fe^{++++}\)

  • Coulumb’s Equation:

    \(F=\frac{QQ^{'}}{\epsilon d^{2}}\)

    \(\epsilon = \epsilon _{0}D\)

  • Gouy-Chapman Equation:

    \(\frac{1}{K}=\sqrt{\frac{\epsilon _{0}D k T}{2n_0e^{2}v{2}}}\)

    Constants:
    \(\epsilon _{0}\) = permittivity of the vacuum;
    \(k=\frac{R}{N_A}\) = Boltzmann constant;
    \(e\) = charge of one electron;

    Relevant variables:
    \(D\) = dielectric constant;
    \(n_0\) = reference ion concentration;
    \(v\) = ion valance;

Interaction between clay particles

  • Repulsion: is caused by DDL interaction, \(V_R\)
  • Atraction: is caused by Van der Waals forces, \(V_A\) A thicker double layer lead to a flocculated fabric, due to an increased repulsion. A thinner DDL lead to a dispersed fabric, in which the particles are aligned with each other.

Influence of system variables

  • Electrolyte concentration
    \(\frac{1}{K} \propto (\frac{1}{n_0})^{0.5}\)
    The thickness of the diffuse double layer decreases as the electrolyte concentration increases.

  • Cation valance
    \(\frac{1}{K} \propto (\frac{1}{v})\)
    The thickness of the diffuse double layer decreases as the cation valance increases.

  • Dielectric constant \(\frac{1}{K} \propto (D)^{0.5}\)
    The thickness of the diffuse double layer decreases as the dielectric constant of the permeant decreases.

  • Radius of cataions \(\frac{1}{K} \propto (\frac{1}{v})\)
    The thickness of the diffuse double layer increases as the cation valance increases.
