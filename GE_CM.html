Clay Minerals

Elements (atoms) combine to form structural units, that together form unit cells (the smallest subdivision of a crystal), which grouped create minerals.

Most common elements in soil:

  • Oxygen \(O^{-2}\)
  • Silicon \(Si^{+4}\)
  • Hydrogen \(H^{+1}\)
  • Nitrogen \(Ni^{+3}\)
  • Aluminum \(Al^{+3}\)
  • Magnesium \(Mg^{+2}\)

Structural Units

  • Silica Tetrahedron* \((SiO_4)^{4-}\)
    • Negatively charged \(\therefore\) needs to combine to satisfy neutrality;
    • Highly charged and well packed;
    • Quartz is an electrically neutral combination of silica tetrahedron \((SiO_4)\).
  • Alumina Octahedron
    • Gibbsite \(Al_2(OH)_6 \to [Al_{2/3}(OH)_6]^{4-}\);
    • Brucite \(Mg_3(OH)_6 \to [Mg(OH)_6]^{4-}\).

Silicates (Mineral)

  • Combinations of silica tetrahedron;
  • The structural units only share one oxygen;
  • Combinations (in order of drecreasing stability):
    • Island;
    • Double island;
    • Ring;
    • Chain;
    • Double chain;
    • Sheets;
    • Frameworks.

Isomorphous Substitution

  • In order to form the clay, the crystals need to undergo isomorphous substitution, which is the replacement of ions of one type by ions of another type, with the same or different valance, but with retention of the same crystal structure (from the greek: iso = same, morphous = form or shape);
  • The new ions may have different valance, therefore negative charges accumulates;
  • Happens during geological time.

Cation Exchange Capacity

  • Is the charge required to neutralize the electronegativity of a given mass of dry clay.
    • Avogadro’s Number: \(N_A = 6.02214 \times 10^{23}\, mol^{-1}\)
    • Atomic Weight: the weight of one mol of an atom or molecule, \(W_a\)
    • Valance: Number of charges in one element, \(Va\)
    • One Equivalent: \(OEq = N_A charges\);
    • \(\# Ions = \frac{\# Eq \times N_A}{Va}\)
    • Equivalent weight (weight per charge): \(W_e = \frac{W}{Va}\);
    • Weight: \(W = \frac{\# Eq}{Va}W_a\);
    • \(\#Eq = \frac{W \times Va}{W_a}\)

  • net negative charge due to isomorphous substitution.

The minerals

  • Kaolinite
    • 1:1 unit cells
    • hydrogen bonding between layers;
    • relatively thick minerals formed by various layers;
    • low plasticity, does not swell;
    • basal space 7.2A
  • Halloysite
    • 1:1 unit cells
    • tubular shape
    • can dehydrate, which is an irreversible process
    • basal space 10A
  • Smectite group
    • 2:1 unit cells
    • high swell potential;
    • Montmorillonite a smectite where every sixth \(Al^{+3}\) has been replaced by a \(Mg^{+2}\);
    • Bentonite is a sodium montmorillonite.
  • Mica like clay minerals
    • 2:1 unit cells;
    • Vermiculite
      • two layers of water between unit cells.
    • Illite
      • most frequent clay in sedimentary soils;
      • has plane of weakness for shearing.

Identifying clay minerals

  • Atterberg Limits

    • Liquid Limit (LL): the water content at which the behavior of the clay changes from the plastic state to the liquid state;

    • Plastic Limit (PL): the water content at which the behavior of the clay changes from the semi-solid to the the plastic state

    • Plasticity Index (PI): is the range of water content at which the behavior of clay remains plastic.
      \(PI = LL - PL\)

    • Plasticity

      • M: inorganic silts;
      • O: Organic
      • L: low plasticity
      • H: high plasticity
  • Activity
    • Measures the potential for swelling
      \(A=\frac{PI}{\% < 2 \mu }\)
  • X-Ray fraction
    • Bragg’s Law
      • Identify the presence of a mineral, but does not measure the proportion of that mineral in a sample; \(n\lambda =2d\sin\theta\)
